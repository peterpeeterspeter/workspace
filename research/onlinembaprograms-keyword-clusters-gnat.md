## Appendix: GNAT Test Methodology

### Overview of GNAT Framework

GNAT (Grammar-Noun-Article Tests) is a keyword quality validation framework designed to filter high-value, natural search queries from awkward or low-intent keywords.

### G - Grammar Tests (33 points)

**Purpose:** Ensure keywords are grammatically correct and represent natural search queries.

**Test Components:**
1. **Subject-Verb Agreement (11 points)**
   - Does the phrase make grammatical sense?
   - Are modifiers in correct positions?
   - Is word order natural for English?

2. **Natural Language Query (11 points)**
   - Would a native speaker type this exact phrase?
   - Does it match real search patterns?
   - Is it conversational or formal when appropriate?

3. **Search Intent Clarity (11 points)**
   - Is the grammatical structure clear?
   - Does the syntax reveal user intent?
   - Is there ambiguity in meaning?

**Scoring:**
- Pass: 33 points (natural, grammatically correct)
- Minor Issues: 20 points (grammatically sound but slightly awkward)
- Fail: 0 points (unnatural, confusing, or broken grammar)

**Examples:**
- "online mba programs" ✅ Pass (33) - Natural, clear
- "mba online programs good" ❌ Fail (0) - Incorrect word order
- "online mba programs best for me" ⚠️ Minor (20) - Conversational but wordy

---

### N - Noun Tests (33 points)

**Purpose:** Verify keywords contain relevant, specific nouns indicating commercial intent.

**Test Components:**
1. **Specificity (11 points)**
   - Are nouns specific to the MBA/education domain?
   - Do they target specific programs, features, or outcomes?
   - Are they overly broad or generic?

2. **Relevance (11 points)**
   - Do nouns align with MBA program research?
   - Are they related to business education?
   - Do they match user intent for program selection?

3. **Commercial Intent (11 points)**
   - Do nouns indicate research/decision behavior?
   - Are they transactional or commercial investigation?
   - Do they suggest purchase/application intent?

**Scoring:**
- Highly Specific MBA Terms: 33 points (MBA, programs, concentrations, etc.)
- Related Business Terms: 20 points (degree, graduate, business school)
- Too General: 0 points (information, stuff, things)

**Examples:**
- "online mba programs" ✅ 33 points - Specific, commercial, relevant
- "online graduate degree" ❌ 0 points - Too general, low intent
- "business graduate programs" ⚠️ 20 points - Related but not MBA-specific

---

### A - Article Tests (34 points)

**Purpose:** Ensure articles (a, an, the) are used naturally or correctly omitted.

**Test Components:**
1. **Article Necessity (11 points)**
   - Are articles needed for natural phrasing?
   - Is the article choice correct (a/an/the)?
   - Would adding/removing articles improve flow?

2. **Article Placement (11 points)**
   - Are articles in natural positions?
   - Do they follow standard English patterns?
   - Is the placement consistent with search behavior?

3. **Query Pattern Matching (12 points)**
   - Does it match real search patterns?
   - Do articles reflect how people actually search?
   - Are they consistent with common search queries?

**Scoring:**
- Natural Usage: 34 points (articles used naturally or correctly omitted)
- Acceptable but Awkward: 20 points (grammatically correct but not optimal)
- Incorrect: 0 points (misused or missing required articles)

**Examples:**
- "online mba programs" ✅ 34 points - No article needed, natural
- "what is the best online mba" ✅ 34 points - "the" is natural here
- "an online mba program" ✅ 34 points - "an" is correct
- "online mba a programs" ❌ 0 points - Article misplaced
- "mba programs online the" ❌ 0 points - Unnatural placement

---

### Quality Score Calculation (0-100)

```
Quality Score = Grammar Score (0-33) + Noun Score (0-33) + Article Score (0-34)
```

**Score Interpretation:**
- **90-100:** Excellent - Natural, specific, high commercial intent
- **80-89:** Very Good - Minor issues but strong overall
- **70-79:** Good - Acceptable but could be optimized
- **60-69:** Fair - Some weaknesses in quality
- **Below 60:** Poor - Not recommended for targeting

### Integration with SEO Strategy

**Why GNAT Works:**
1. **Grammar:** Ensures content matches user search patterns
2. **Nouns:** Focuses on commercial-intent keywords, not just informational
3. **Articles:** Validates natural language for voice search and featured snippets

**When to Use GNAT:**
- Keyword research validation
- Content optimization for natural language
- Voice search optimization
- Featured snippet targeting

**Limitations:**
- Doesn't account for search volume or competition
- Doesn't measure keyword difficulty
- Must be combined with opportunity analysis

**Best Practices:**
- Use GNAT for filtering, not primary keyword discovery
- Combine with volume/KD data for prioritization
- Apply to long-tail keywords especially
- Re-test annually as search patterns evolve
